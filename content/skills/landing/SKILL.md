# Landing page

A single marketing page that reads as designed, not generated. Its job is to make one
audience believe one thing and take one action. Work in phases — research the argument,
structure it, then build it.

## Research

Before designing, study (use the research/ report if one exists):

- 3–4 real landing pages for products in this exact category, plus two from adjacent
  categories worth borrowing structure from. Note what earns attention and what reads
  as filler.
- The audience's real objections and the words they use — reviews, forums, the product's
  own community. The copy must answer real doubts, not recite generic benefits.
- The one true differentiator: what this product does that the alternatives do not, said
  in a sentence a user would recognise.

## Structure

Compose the sections the *argument* needs — a landing is a sequence of claims, each
earned by the one before it. Lead the hero with the differentiator, not a generic
value-prop; every section below the fold either advances the argument or is cut.

Ship at least one section that isn't in the default playbook — a full-bleed quote, a
comparison against the status quo, an inline mini-demo, a single product detail rendered
large, or a "how it works" that actually shows the product. A page that runs
hero → features → pricing → FAQ → CTA in that order, unvaried, is the tell to avoid.

## The distinctive move

Name the one thing only someone who used this product would put on the page — a real
screenshot, a real number, a real interaction, a sentence in the founder's actual voice.
That is the ~20% that gives the page soul. If you cannot name it, the brief is
underspecified: commit to the most likely one rather than hedging into template copy.

## Craft

- Microcopy carries a point of view: "Start tracking" beats "Get started".
- Pull real product details and real numbers. If a number is genuinely unknown, use a
  labelled placeholder — never invent one.
- Lead with type and layout; reach for a hero visual only when it earns its place.
