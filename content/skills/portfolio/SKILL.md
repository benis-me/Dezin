# Portfolio

A portfolio's job is to make one audience — a hiring manager, a client — want to reach
out. Research who that is and what they need to see, structure the proof, then build it.

## Research

- Who is hiring or buying, and what they scan for in the first ten seconds. A recruiter, a
  design lead, and a founder weight different things.
- The two or three pieces of work that actually make the case, and the real outcomes
  behind them — numbers, roles, what changed. Weak work dilutes strong work; cut it.
- One or two portfolios in this field that feel distinctive (not templated), for what to
  borrow and what to avoid.

## Structure

- A tight intro: who you are and what you do, one line, with a memorable type move — not a
  generic tagline.
- A responsive **project grid** (2–3 columns desktop, 1 mobile). Each card: a framed
  thumbnail, title, role, year.
- The lead project opens a **case study**: problem → approach → outcome, with real
  artifacts and figures. Cover the empty state ("no projects yet") and image loading with
  a neutral placeholder frame, never a broken box.

## The distinctive move

The one thing that makes this person unmistakable — a signature type treatment, a way of
showing process, a single project told exceptionally well. Let the work carry the color;
keep chrome neutral with one accent. Real project names and outcomes only.
